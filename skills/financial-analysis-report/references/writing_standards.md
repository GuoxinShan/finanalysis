# Financial Analysis Writing Standards

## Core Philosophy

**Your Goal**: Create insightful, customized analysis that demonstrates understanding of the business, not just describe numbers.

## Key Principles

1. **Explain WHY, not just WHAT** - Numbers tell a story; interpret it
2. **Be specific, not generic** - Use actual data and examples
3. **Connect the dots** - Link insights across sections
4. **Be forward-looking** - Assess implications and risks
5. **Write for the user** - Professional but accessible

## Do's

✅ **Be specific**: "Revenue grew 58% YoY to RM3.25b" not "Revenue increased significantly"

✅ **Explain drivers**: "Margin compression due to mix shift toward lower-margin East Malaysian operations" not "Margins declined"

✅ **Connect insights**: "This working capital drag explains negative operating cash flow despite accounting profits"

✅ **Use data wisely**: Don't just list numbers - interpret them

✅ **Be forward-looking**: Assess implications, not just describe history

✅ **Write clearly**: Professional but accessible, avoid jargon

## Don'ts

❌ **Don't hedge excessively**: "Revenue may have possibly increased somewhat" - make clear judgments

❌ **Don't be generic**: "The company performed well" - what does "well" mean?

❌ **Don't ignore negatives**: Balanced analysis includes risks and concerns

❌ **Don't over-template**: Customize insights to the specific company, not generic statements

❌ **Don't describe without analyzing**: "Revenue was X, PBT was Y" - so what?

## Writing Style

- Use imperative form in instructions
- Lead with the most important insight
- Support claims with specific numbers
- Be balanced (strengths AND concerns)
- Avoid hedging - make clear judgments

## Example: Good vs. Bad

**Bad**:
> The company's revenue increased from the prior year. Operating margin also improved. The company is in a good financial position.

**Good**:
> Revenue surged 58% YoY to RM3.25b, driven by East Malaysia expansion which contributed 40% of incremental sales, though this growth came at the expense of margins (new markets: 8-10% vs. Peninsula: 15-18%), raising questions about growth quality.

## Quality Checklist

Before submitting your sections, verify:

**Content Quality**:

## Professional Tone Standards

### Why Tone Matters

Financial analysis serves diverse stakeholders (executives, investors, regulators). Professional tone:
- Builds credibility and trust
- Maintains objectivity while providing clear judgments
- Ensures accessibility without sacrificing rigor
- Avoids emotional language that could undermine analysis

### Professional Language (DO)

✅ **DO use precise, neutral language:**
- "Revenue declined 12% YoY" (factual, specific)
- "Margin compression reflects competitive pressures" (analytical)
- "Working capital management requires attention" (constructive)
- "Cash flow generation remains weak relative to peers" (objective assessment)

✅ **DO make clear judgments with evidence:**
- "The 15% revenue growth is impressive but quality is questionable given margin erosion"
- "Debt levels are manageable at 0.8x net debt-to-equity, though rising interest costs warrant monitoring"

### Language to Avoid (DON'T)

❌ **DON'T use emotional or sensational language:**
- ~~"Revenue collapsed dramatically"~~ → "Revenue declined sharply"
- ~~"Disastrous quarter"~~ → "Challenging quarter with multiple headwinds"
- ~~"Amazing growth"~~ → "Strong growth of X%"
- ~~"Terrible cash flow"~~ → "Negative operating cash flow of RM X million"

❌ **DON'T hedge excessively:**
- ~~"Revenue might have possibly increased somewhat"~~ → "Revenue increased X%"
- ~~"It could be suggested that margins appear to potentially be under pressure"~~ → "Margins are under pressure due to X"

❌ **DON'T use colloquialisms or informal language:**
- ~~"The company is sitting on a pile of cash"~~ → "The company holds substantial cash reserves"
- ~~"They're burning through cash"~~ → "Cash consumption remains high at RM X million/quarter"
- ~~"The business is booming"~~ → "Revenue grew X% YoY, driven by Y"

### Balanced Analysis

Professional tone doesn't mean avoiding negative assessments. Be direct about concerns:

**Example - Addressing Weaknesses:**
> "Operating cash flow remained negative for the third consecutive year (-RM 45m), primarily due to working capital build-up in inventory (+RM 30m) and receivables (+RM 20m). While revenue growth is strong, the inability to convert profits to cash raises sustainability questions."

This is:
- ✅ Specific (quantifies the issue)
- ✅ Analytical (explains drivers)
- ✅ Objective (states facts without emotional language)
- ✅ Forward-looking (raises sustainability concern)

## Data Presentation Standards

### Why Standards Matter

Consistent data presentation:
- Reduces cognitive load for readers
- Prevents misinterpretation of figures
- Maintains professional appearance
- Ensures accuracy across sections

### 1. Handling Missing Data

**Rule**: Use dash (`-`) for missing values, never "N/A", "None", or "0" when data is unavailable.

**Rationale**: "N/A" is ambiguous (not applicable vs. not available), while "-" clearly indicates missing data.

**Examples:**
```
Revenue Growth:     15.2%   (2024) | 12.8% (2023) | -    (2022*)
Margin:            18.5%   (2024) | 20.1% (2023) | 19.3% (2022)
* Prior year data not available
```

**In text:**
- ✅ "Comparable prior year data is not available for this segment"
- ❌ "N/A for prior year"
- ❌ "No data for 2022"

### 2. Currency Formatting

**Rule**: Always use "RM" (Ringgit Malaysia) in text, never "MYR" (currency code).

**Rationale**: "RM" is the standard written abbreviation in Malaysian business contexts. "MYR" is for technical/coding contexts (ISO 4217).

**Examples:**
- ✅ "Revenue grew to RM 3.25 billion"
- ✅ "Net profit of RM 45.2 million"
- ❌ "Revenue of MYR 3.25b"
- ❌ "Net profit: MYR 45.2m"

**In tables:**
- Column headers: "RM'000" or "RM million" (consistent across report)
- Text: "RM 3.25 billion" (space between RM and number)

### 3. Basis Points Notation

**Rule**: Use "bps" (basis points) for changes < 1 percentage point, "ppt" (percentage points) for changes ≥ 1 percentage point.

**Rationale**: Precision matters. 50 basis points (0.50%) is different from 50 percentage points.

**Conversion:**
- 100 bps = 1 percentage point (ppt)
- 1 bp = 0.01%

**Examples:**
- ✅ "Margin improved 25 bps to 18.5%" (small change)
- ✅ "Margin declined 2.3 ppt to 18.5%" (large change)
- ❌ "Margin improved 0.25%" (ambiguous - could mean 0.25ppt or 25bps)
- ❌ "Margin increased 2.3%" (when you mean 2.3ppt from 16.2% to 18.5%)

**In text:**
- "Net interest margin improved 15 bps YoY to 2.35%"
- "Operating margin contracted 1.8 ppt due to higher input costs"

### 4. Negative Values in Tables

**Rule**: Use parentheses `()` for negative values in tables, never minus signs `-`.

**Rationale**: Parentheses are the standard accounting convention for negative values and prevent confusion with dashes used for missing data.

**Examples:**

| Metric | 2024 | 2023 |
|--------|------|------|
| Operating Cash Flow (RM'000) | (45,230) | (32,100) |
| Net Debt | (150,000) | (200,000) |

**In text:**
- ✅ "Operating cash flow was negative RM 45.2 million"
- ✅ "Net cash position of RM 150 million" (positive, no parentheses needed)
- ❌ "Operating cash flow: -RM 45.2 million"

### 5. Number Formatting Consistency

**Thousands separator**: Always use commas (e.g., 1,234,567 not 1234567)

**Decimal places**: Be consistent within sections
- Currency amounts: 0 decimal places for large numbers, 2 for precision needed
- Percentages: 1 decimal place standard (e.g., 15.2%, not 15.23%)
- Ratios: 2 decimal places (e.g., 1.25x, not 1.3x)

**Abbreviations**: Define on first use
- "RM 3.25b" → "RM 3.25 billion" on first mention, then "RM 3.25b" acceptable
- "bps" → "basis points (bps)" on first mention

### 6. Unit Consistency

**Rule**: Maintain consistent units within comparisons and tables.

**Examples:**
- ✅ "Revenue: RM 3.25b (2024) vs RM 2.06b (2023)" - same unit
- ❌ "Revenue: RM 3.25b (2024) vs RM 2,060m (2023)" - mixed units

**Exception**: When highlighting scale differences:
- "Revenue grew from RM 50 million (2019) to RM 3.25 billion (2024)" - acceptable to show dramatic growth

## 7. Calculation Precision Standards

**Rule**: Calculate from source data BEFORE rounding. Never calculate from pre-rounded values.

**Rationale**: Rounding before calculating compounds errors through cascading operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division).

**Pattern - The Rounding Error Cascade:**

❌ **WRONG** - Round first, then calculate:
```
PAT (rounded):    215.5 (from 215,492)
PATMI (rounded):  114.8 (from 114,818)
NCI = 215.5 - 114.8 = 100.7  ← WRONG (actual is 97,078 → 97.1)
Error: 3.6 million
```

✅ **CORRECT** - Calculate first, then round:
```
NCI (actual): 215,492 - 114,818 = 97,078
NCI (rounded): 97.078 → 97.1  ← CORRECT
NCI %: 97,078 / 215,492 = 45.06% → 45.1%
```

**Safe Operations:**
- ✅ Percentage from source: `value / base` (calculate with raw numbers, then round result)
- ✅ Difference from source: `value1 - value2` (subtract raw numbers, then round)
- ✅ Growth rate from source: `(current - prior) / prior` (use raw, then round)

**Dangerous Operations:**
- ❌ Percentage from rounded: `rounded_value / rounded_base` (compounds rounding errors)
- ❌ Difference from rounded: `rounded1 - rounded2` (error = rounding_error1 - rounding_error2)
- ❌ Growth from rounded: `(rounded_current - rounded_prior) / rounded_prior` (triple error source)

**Examples:**

**Minority Interest Calculation:**
```markdown
# Source data (RM'000):
PAT: 215,492
PATMI: 114,818
NCI: 97,078 (calculated)

# Display format:
✅ "Profit attribution to minority interests: 45.1% (RM97.1m of RM215.5m)"
   - Percentage: 97,078 / 215,492 = 45.06% → 45.1%
   - NCI amount: 97,078 / 1000 = 97.078 → 97.1
   - PAT amount: 215,492 / 1000 = 215.492 → 215.5

❌ "Profit attribution to minority interests: 46.7% (RM100.7m of RM215.5m)"
   - Wrong NCI: 215.5 - 114.8 = 100.7 (rounded subtraction)
   - Wrong percentage: 100.7 / 215.5 = 46.7% (from wrong NCI)
   - Error magnitude: 1.6 percentage points
```

**Best Practices:**
1. Always calculate from the raw fs_index.json values (RM'000 precision)
2. Round ONLY the final result for display
3. Never use rounded table values as inputs to new calculations
4. When in doubt, recalculate from source data

**Verification:**
- If your derived metric (e.g., NCI) doesn't match fs_index.json, you're calculating wrong
- Check: Is the source value available in fs_index? Use it directly, don't derive from other rounded values
- Cross-check percentages: Calculate both ways (raw vs. rounded) to verify they match within 0.1%
